# Research and Stats: Use With Care

Every figure here is perishable. **Last reviewed 2026-06-18.** AI citation behavior shifts fast, and different studies measure different things under the same labels. Before quoting any number to a user, verify it is still current and state the date. Prefer principles over precise figures in client-facing work.

## The Princeton GEO study

The foundational peer-reviewed work is "GEO: Generative Engine Optimization" (Aggarwal et al.), presented at ACM KDD 2024, with authors from Princeton, Georgia Tech, IIT Delhi, and the Allen Institute for AI. It introduced the GEO-Bench benchmark and tested optimization methods across roughly 10,000 queries.

Headline finding: GEO techniques can boost visibility in generative responses by up to about 40 percent. Two caveats that matter more than the headline:

1. **Effects vary heavily by domain.** The study explicitly found that the best method depends on the topic. Statistics and citations were strongest for scientific and technical content; fluency and readability were stronger for general-interest topics. There is no single universal ranking of methods. Do not present the method list as a fixed leaderboard.

2. **Lower-ranked pages gain most.** Pages around position five and below saw the largest lift (reported up to roughly 115 percent), while pages already at position one saw little change. So GEO is most valuable to challengers, not incumbents.

Approximate per-method effects from the paper, to be treated as directional, not exact: adding statistics and citing sources were among the strongest; adding expert quotations helped; keyword stuffing performed poorly. Use these to choose tactics, not to promise percentages.

## What suppresses citation

This is the most actionable and most overlooked area.

- **Promotional tone reduces citation.** Industry measurement (for example Semrush analyses through 2026) has found promotional or "salesy" language meaningfully lowers AI citation rates. Removing marketing language is frequently higher leverage than adding any tactic.
- **Question-and-answer formatting raises citation.** Structuring content as direct question-and-answer blocks measurably improves citation rates.
- **Keyword stuffing actively hurts** in generative contexts, unlike classic SEO where it merely fails to help.

## Citation distribution and volatility

Two patterns to keep front of mind, both well supported across 2025 to 2026 studies:

- **The distribution is a long tail.** Across large datasets, even the most-cited single domain rarely exceeds a few percent of total citations, and the large majority of citations spread across thousands of domains. The implication: winning AI visibility is mostly about the long tail of relevant, well-structured sources, not capturing a handful of giant ones.
- **Share is volatile.** Domain citation share can swing dramatically within weeks. Documented example: one platform's citation of a major community site moved from a majority of responses to a small fraction inside roughly a month in late 2025, as the provider adjusted to avoid over-relying on single sources. Build strategy assuming volatility is the normal condition.

## A note on conflicting numbers

Citation statistics are frequently misquoted because two different metrics get the same label:

- **Share of total citations**: of all citations counted, what fraction went to a domain. These numbers are small (often low single digits even for top domains).
- **Frequency of appearance**: in what fraction of responses a domain shows up at least once. These numbers are much larger (sometimes tens of percent).

These are not comparable, and mixing them produces nonsense like "domain X is 2 percent and also 40 percent of citations." When a source quotes a citation percentage, identify which metric it is before using it, and never place the two side by side as if they describe the same thing.
